## Campaign Identity

One-sentence premise:

Alex and Guide Mira explore a hidden jungle valley, repair an old Sun Compass, and use it to open the quiet Sun Temple before the last beam of light disappears.

Player character:

Alex, a brave junior explorer. Alex is curious, kind, and careful. Alex solves problems by looking closely, using items, helping people, and reading simple clues.

Companion or guide:

Guide Mira, a local explorer and teacher. Mira gives gentle hints, explains map travel, and helps keep the English text short and safe.

Main mystery:

The Sun Compass at base camp is missing its bright lenses. Without the lenses, the old map cannot show the path to the Sun Temple. Each lens is connected to a place in the valley and lights a new part of the map.

Main obstacle:

The valley itself is the obstacle: broken bridges, dark caves, locked stone gates, missing tools, sleepy paths, and old machines that need the right item. There is no rival explorer in the planned campaign.

Ending:

Alex restores the Sun Compass, opens the temple observatory, and turns the mirror so sunlight reaches the valley garden again. The ending should feel like discovery and repair, not combat.

Tone:

Warm jungle adventure, bright mystery, safe danger, practical problem-solving, a little wonder. No weapons, horror, traps that feel violent, real franchise marks, or adult thriller tone.

Non-goals:

- No grammar boss fights.
- No worksheet-style level structure.
- No props that exist only because a prompt produced them.
- No complex language outside `curriculum-scope.md` in player-facing text.
- No asset generation before the dependency graph and prop budget exist.

## Design Promise

The child should feel:

- "I found a new place."
- "I know what this item is for."
- "I can go back and use this now."
- "The map is growing."
- "The English helps me understand the adventure."

The child should not feel:

- "This is a grammar test with scenery."
- "I am collecting random things."
- "The game made a lot of objects but only three matter."

## Plot Outline

### Beat 1: The Broken Sun Compass

Alex arrives at base camp. Mira shows an old Sun Compass on a table. The compass is open, but several lens slots are empty. A torn valley map is next to it.

The valley problem is visible from the start: a small sun flower near camp is drooping because the old light path is broken. Keep the line simple if used in dialogue: `The flower needs sun.`

Adventure purpose:

- introduce the hub;
- introduce the map;
- establish the missing lenses;
- show that the valley needs sunlight again;
- let the player take a small starter item such as a backpack or field notebook;
- unlock the first nearby screen.

### Beat 2: The First Map Piece

Alex explores the camp edge and finds a map piece caught under a fallen branch. The child can inspect and move the branch directly; no separate starter tool is needed.

Adventure purpose:

- teach inspect/take behavior;
- create the first return-to-camp moment;
- combine the torn map and map piece into a usable valley map, with the torn edge and river line visibly joining;
- unlock the jungle path marker on the map.

### Beat 3: The Broken Bridge

The jungle path leads to a small river bridge. The bridge is broken. Alex can see a bright object on the far side, but cannot cross yet.

The far side also shows Lina's lost basket, visible but unreachable until the bridge is safe.

Adventure purpose:

- require an inventory item such as rope;
- show a clear blocked route;
- make the world feel connected;
- unlock river-side exploration after repair.

### Beat 4: The Village Helper

Across the river is a small village garden. A friendly NPC needs help finding useful items after wind moved them around. Helping the NPC reveals where the first Sun Compass lens is hidden and gives Alex a clear hint.

Lina needs the basket because her seeds are inside it. This keeps the NPC beat concrete: the basket matters to the garden, not only to the puzzle graph.

Adventure purpose:

- introduce giving an item to an NPC;
- make the NPC interaction practical rather than quiz-like;
- connect basket -> seeds -> flower pot -> Green Lens into one small story beat;
- use natural language around possession and place without a drill.

### Beat 5: The Dark Cave

After Alex places the Green Lens in the Sun Compass, green light touches the repaired map and reveals a Waterfall Cave marker. The cave is too dark. Alex must combine two simple items to make a light source.

Adventure purpose:

- introduce combine/use item gating;
- give a strong reason to revisit camp or village;
- hide a second lens or a temple key inside.

### Beat 6: The Stone Courtyard

The Blue Lens reveals Temple Steps. Alex finds an empty stone cup near a dry sun channel, fills it at the already-known waterfall, and returns to send water through the stone gate. The opened gate leads to a sun courtyard, where a stone flower reveals the third lens.

Adventure purpose:

- make the child reuse a known place for a new reason;
- use one visible water cause-and-effect chain;
- make the temple feel earned without a symbol quiz;
- reveal the Stone Lens as a result of the opened water path.

### Beat 7: The Observatory Door

Inside the temple, the Sun Compass fits into a central pedestal, but it still needs the final lens. The map now shows a high observatory path.

Adventure purpose:

- use the world map as a real navigation tool;
- give a late-game reason to travel back through unlocked places;
- set up the finale without a grammar test.

### Final Beat: The Sun Mirror

Alex places the complete Sun Compass in the observatory. The sun mirror turns. Light reaches the valley garden again. Mira thanks Alex and the map changes from torn and dark to complete and bright.

Adventure purpose:

- final inventory use;
- visible world-state change;
- satisfying ending;
- optional recap using simple, curriculum-safe lines.

## Key Story Objects

| Object | Story role | Gameplay role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sun Compass | Central mystery object | Tracks lens progress and later opens the observatory. |
| Torn map | Starts incomplete | Becomes world map as places are discovered; repaired edges and river lines visibly join. |
| Five compass lenses | Main progress items | Each lens lights a new map area or temple mechanism; all five unlock the final mirror. |
| Rope | Practical tool | Repairs bridge or helps cross a gap. |
| Torch or lamp | Practical tool | Opens dark cave. |
| Field notebook | Optional collection frame | Holds simple clues, translations, and found notes. |
| Lina's basket | Village story item | Holds seeds; returning it lets Lina share the flower-pot clue. |

## Adventure Style Rules

- Every major object must be useful at least once.
- Every region should have a reason to return.
- Every NPC should want or give something concrete.
- Every new map marker should be discovered through play.
- Every lens placement should visibly reveal or change something.
- Recurring sun symbols should mark important old valley objects.
- The game should prefer "use the rope on the bridge" over "answer a rope quiz."
- Hints can use Bulgarian support, but progression should come from adventure actions.

## Language Style For Player-Facing Text

These are examples of the intended line style, not final content:

- `This is the Sun Compass.`
- `It has three empty spots.`
- `I have a rope.`
- `Use the rope on the bridge.`
- `The bridge is safe now.`
- `There is a map piece under the branch.`
- `Mira is waiting near the camp table.`
- `Give the basket to Lina.`
- `The cave is dark.`
- `The mirror is moving.`

Keep final game text inside the grammar and vocabulary boundaries from `curriculum-scope.md`.

## Locked Decisions

- The first playable vertical slice includes only Base Camp, Camp Edge, Jungle Path, Broken Bridge, and Village Garden.
- The map UI unlocks after the player finds the torn map.
- The first useful travel marker unlocks after the player combines the torn map with the first map piece.
- The first version includes item combining.
- There is no rival explorer; the adventure is obstacle-only.
- The full campaign uses five Sun Compass lenses for a longer adventure.
