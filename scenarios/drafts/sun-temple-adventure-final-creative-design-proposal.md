# Sun Temple Adventure: Final Creative Design Proposal

## Document Purpose

This document defines the recommended creative direction for improving and completing `Sun Temple Adventure`.

It is intended for use by writers, game designers, content designers, artists, and LLMs generating scenario content, dialogue, visual states, or implementation specifications.

## Experience Definition

### Target players

- Children aged 9–12.
- English learners playing a point-and-click adventure.

### Player fantasy

The player is a curious junior explorer who discovers how a forgotten jungle system once carried water and sunlight through the Sun Temple. By observing the environment, helping people, remembering useful places, and repairing old mechanisms, the player brings sunlight back to the valley garden.

### Core emotional promise

> I am uncovering how this valley works and helping bring it back to life.

### Central dramatic question

> Why has sunlight stopped reaching the valley garden, and how can Alex restore it?

### Tone

- Warm and adventurous.
- Curious rather than frightening.
- Mysterious without becoming dark or threatening.
- Practical and grounded in visible cause and effect.
- Non-combat-focused.
- Hopeful, with restoration as the final reward.

## Core Story Direction

At Base Camp, Alex notices that a small sun flower no longer receives sunlight from the direction of the temple. Mira is studying the incomplete Sun Compass, but neither Alex nor Mira initially understands exactly how the Compass, the temple, and the missing sunlight are connected.

Alex restores a torn map, repairs a bridge, and returns Lina's lost basket of seeds. Lina reveals the Green Lens. When Alex places it in the Sun Compass, the Compass reveals a route toward Waterfall Cave.

Alex combines a jar and glowing leaf to light a dark cave, discovers an old key, and opens a stone box containing the Blue Lens. Its placement reveals Temple Steps and suggests that the Compass is mapping parts of an older connected system.

At Temple Steps, Alex recognizes that a dry carved channel needs water. Alex returns to the waterfall, fills a stone cup, and pours the water into the channel. The water opens the temple gate and activates a stone sun flower in the courtyard. Alex retrieves the Stone Lens.

When the Stone Lens is placed, the three lenses point toward Mirror Hall. Alex and Mira realize that light may exist inside the temple in a form they do not yet understand.

In Mirror Hall, Alex redirects a beam through a readable sequence of mirrors. The solved light path reveals the Mirror Lens and opens the route toward the observatory.

Alex earns the Sun Lens on the final observatory approach and completes the Sun Compass. The complete Compass opens the Observatory Door or activates its final mechanism.

At the Sun Observatory, Alex turns the great sun mirror. Sunlight travels through the restored temple system and reaches the valley garden. The sun flower rises, Lina's plants turn toward the light, and the restored map becomes complete and bright.

## Story Principles

### Discovery before explanation

The player should see a change, clue, or connection before a character explains its full meaning.

Characters may:

- notice visible details;
- express uncertainty;
- form partial theories;
- react to discoveries;
- provide concise help after the player is stuck.

Characters should not:

- explain the entire temple system in the opening;
- announce a puzzle solution before the player can reason about it;
- interpret every discovery on the player's behalf.

### Physical meaning before symbolic meaning

Each lens is a functional part of the Sun Compass and temple system. Its identity comes from the place, mechanism, and environmental connection associated with it.

| Lens | Functional identity | Adventure connection |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Green Lens | Reveals the living river and garden route | Lina, seeds, flowers, and the waterfall region |
| Blue Lens | Reveals routes hidden by darkness or water | Glowing leaf, Dark Cave, stone key, and blue box |
| Stone Lens | Reveals or activates ancient temple channels | Waterfall water, stone cup, channel, gate, and courtyard flower |
| Mirror Lens | Redirects or interprets reflected light | Mirror Hall and the inner light path |
| Sun Lens | Focuses and completes the Compass | Observatory approach and final sun mechanism |

Kindness, curiosity, memory, and persistence should emerge from Alex's actions. They should not be presented as explicit moral labels attached to the lenses.

### Visible cause and effect

Important actions should alter the world visibly:

- The rope remains tied to the repaired bridge.
- The cave entrance glows after it is lit.
- The blue box remains open.
- Water visibly follows the temple channel.
- The gate opens and remains open.
- The stone sun flower responds to water.
- Lens placements visibly change the Compass and map.
- Mirror alignment visibly redirects a beam.
- The final mirror sends sunlight across the valley.
- The garden visibly recovers.

### Connected locations

Locations should feel like parts of one valley rather than isolated puzzle rooms.

Connections should be established through:

- reused resources, especially waterfall water;
- consistent but readable architectural motifs;
- environmental changes visible in more than one location;
- dialogue that refers to places the player has visited;
- a map that grows with the player's understanding;
- final sunlight passing through previously discovered regions.

## Character Direction

## Alex

### Role

Alex is the player's voice inside the world: a curious, kind, careful junior explorer.

### Personality

- Observant.
- Practical.
- Willing to experiment.
- Gently humorous.
- Excited by unusual natural and mechanical discoveries.
- Increasingly confident as the adventure progresses.

### Voice rules

- Use short, concrete sentences.
- Prefer active verbs.
- React to meaningful discoveries rather than every pickup.
- Do not solve a puzzle aloud before the player solves it.
- Allow occasional playful lines when they do not carry essential instructions.
- Keep mandatory dialogue accessible to English learners.

### Character progression

| Stage | Alex's attitude |
| --- | --- |
| Base Camp | Curious about the flower, Compass, and missing sunlight |
| Village route | Helpful and practical; begins connecting actions to people |
| Cave | Cautious but eager to investigate hidden places |
| Temple Steps | Confident enough to remember and reuse a previous location |
| Mirror Hall | Forms theories about light rather than waiting for Mira's answer |
| Observatory | Understands the system and performs the final restoration action |

### Key reaction lines

Broken Bridge, seeing the basket:

> A basket on the other side. Someone must be looking for it.

Glow leaf:

> This leaf is glowing—and it isn't even warm.

Temple channel activation:

> The water is following the sun marks!

Stone Lens placement:

> But there is no sunlight inside the temple... is there?

Final observatory action:

> The light path is complete. Let's send it home.

## Mira

### Role

Mira is a calm expedition guide and investigative companion. She frames the journey, safeguards the Base Camp hub, and helps Alex interpret the Compass without knowing every answer in advance.

### Personality

- Calm.
- Encouraging.
- Attentive to Alex's ideas.
- Knowledgeable about maps and expeditions.
- Curious about the temple's original purpose.
- Never a quiz dispenser.

### Knowledge progression

| Story point | Mira's understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Opening | The Compass is incomplete; the flower's missing sunlight may be connected to the temple |
| Green Lens | Each lens reveals an old route |
| Blue Lens | The routes appear to form parts of one system |
| Stone Lens | The lenses point toward Mirror Hall, but the inner light source remains unexplained |
| Mirror Lens | The temple redirected light toward the observatory route |
| Sun Lens | The complete Compass can activate the final observatory mechanism |
| Ending | Alex has restored the system that carried sunlight to the valley |

### Delivery rules

- Mira does not deliver a speech after every lens.
- Some discoveries are communicated visually.
- Some inferences belong to Alex.
- Additional explanation may be available through optional conversation.
- Mira's required lines should advance the mystery rather than repeat the map state.

## Lina

### Role

Lina gives the first lens sequence a human purpose. Her seeds, flowers, and garden connect everyday life in the valley to the larger sunlight system.

### Character function

- Gives emotional meaning to the basket.
- Establishes that helping the valley's people and living things is part of exploration.
- Connects the Green Lens to growth and the waterfall region.
- Returns in the ending so the player's first act of help receives a final payoff.

### Voice

- Direct and warm.
- Concerned about her seeds without sounding desperate.
- Knowledgeable about local plants.
- Surprised by the older temple mechanisms.

### Ending line

> The flowers are turning toward the temple again. You brought the sunlight back.

## Dialogue And Language Design

### Dialogue budget

Use dialogue selectively so it supports play rather than interrupting it.

Each major region should normally contain:

- one short arrival or discovery reaction;
- one clear required puzzle clue;
- one reaction to the main state change;
- optional inspect lines for additional atmosphere.

Routine inventory pickups need only a confirmation when the resulting state is not visually obvious.

### Required clue structure

Required clues should use:

- concrete nouns;
- active verbs;
- short sentences;
- visible referents;
- one idea per line.

Example:

> The channel is dry.

Not:

> It appears that this ancient mechanism may once have depended upon water flowing through these carved channels.

### Progressive hint structure

#### First hint: visible observation

> The channel is completely dry.

#### Second hint: useful relationship

> These marks look like paths for water.

#### Final hint: remembered location

> The waterfall could fill this cup.

### Optional flavour

Optional inspect dialogue may use slightly richer language when it is not necessary for progress.

Example:

> Whoever built this place liked secrets.

### Dialogue distinction

Required clues, character reactions, and optional flavour should be visually or structurally distinguishable so the player knows which text is actionable.

## Opening Revision: Base Camp

### Story objective

Establish three things without explaining their complete relationship:

1. Sunlight no longer reaches the camp flower.
2. The Sun Compass is incomplete.
3. Alex and Mira suspect that the temple and Compass may be connected to the change.

### Visible state

- A small sun flower near Base Camp is drooping.
- The flower faces toward the distant temple or the direction from which sunlight once arrived.
- The Sun Compass has five empty lens positions.
- The torn map remains available as the first progression object.

### Opening interaction

Inspecting the flower:

Alex:

> This flower is leaning away from the shade.

Mira:

> It always faced the temple. Now the light never reaches it.

Inspecting the Compass:

Mira:

> Five lenses are missing. The Compass changed when the light faded.

Alex:

> Then the lenses may show us what happened.

### Design rule

Do not explain the observatory, mirror system, or complete light path in the opening.

## Current Playable Content: Scene Recommendations

### 1. Base Camp

#### Keep

- Sun Compass.
- Mira beside the table.
- Torn map.
- Supply Tent and Camp Edge routes.

#### Add or revise

- Establish the drooping sun flower.
- Introduce the missing-sunlight mystery.
- Let Alex propose investigating the lenses.
- Keep the immediate action clear: take the torn map and explore the camp.

### 2. Supply Tent

#### Keep

- Rope as the single required tool.
- Simple route back to Base Camp.

#### Add or revise

- Give the rope a concise practical description.
- Use one optional detail to suggest Mira and Alex prepared for exploration.

Optional inspection:

> Mira packs for every problem. Almost every problem.

### 3. Camp Edge

#### Keep

- Fallen branch.
- Hidden map piece.
- Jungle Path locked until the map is restored.

#### Add or revise

- Show a visible corner of the map piece beneath the branch.
- Make moving the branch feel like a deliberate action rather than a repeated inspection.
- Preserve the map-combining interaction.

Alex before moving the branch:

> A piece of paper is caught underneath.

### 4. Jungle Path

#### Keep

- Short navigation beat.
- Sun stone as a route marker.
- Broken Bridge destination.

#### Add or revise

- Use the sun stone to establish a visual motif associated with old routes.
- Keep the motif simple and non-actionable at this stage.
- Let Alex notice that the route was deliberately marked.

Alex:

> Someone marked this path with a sun.

### 5. Broken Bridge

#### Keep

- Unsafe bridge.
- Rope repair.
- Basket visible before it can be reached.
- Permanent tied-rope state.

#### Add or revise

- Let Alex recognize that the basket belongs to someone before meeting Lina.
- Preserve the strong visual goal across the bridge.

Alex:

> A basket on the other side. Someone must be looking for it.

After repair:

> Safe enough for careful explorers.

### 6. Village Garden

#### Keep

- Lina.
- Basket and seeds.
- Flower pot.
- Green Lens.
- Keeper Hut access after helping Lina.

#### Add or revise

- Give the seeds a clear personal meaning.
- Let Lina connect the local plants to unusual light near the waterfall.
- Do not make Lina explain the larger temple mystery.

Before help:

> My seed basket fell near the old bridge. I need it before the planting beds dry out.

After receiving the basket:

> My seeds are safe. Thank you, Alex.

Green Lens clue:

> I found something green and bright beneath that flower pot. I think it belongs to your Compass.

Jar clue:

> There is an empty jar in the hut. Some waterfall leaves glow after sunset.

### 7. Green Lens Placement

#### Visible response

- The Green Lens appears in the Compass.
- A green light or line reveals Waterfall Mouth on the map.
- The effect is concise and immediately readable.

#### Dialogue

Mira:

> The lens revealed an old route beside the water.

Alex:

> So the Compass is not pointing north. It is rebuilding the map.

### 8. Keeper Hut

#### Keep

- Empty jar.
- Shelf of small plants.

#### Add or revise

- Connect the shelf plants to Lina's knowledge of local light-bearing leaves.
- Keep the jar as the only required inventory item.

Optional shelf inspection:

> These plants close in the shade and open in sunlight.

Jar pickup:

> An empty jar. It could carry something small.

### 9. Waterfall Mouth

#### Keep

- Waterfall and pool.
- Glowing leaf.
- Dark cave entrance.
- Jar-and-leaf combination.
- Later water-source reuse.

#### Add or revise

- Emphasize the contrast between the bright water and dark cave.
- Give Alex one reaction to the glowing leaf.
- Preserve the pool as a visible, memorable resource before the cup puzzle begins.

Glow leaf:

> This leaf is glowing—and it isn't even warm.

Cave entrance:

> The light stops at the entrance. I need a way to carry it inside.

After creating the glow jar:

> The jar holds the glow.

### 10. Dark Cave

#### Keep

- Lit cave state.
- Hidden stone key.
- Blue stone box.
- Blue Lens reward.

#### Add or revise

- Give the key location a subtle visible seam or marking.
- Let inspection reveal evidence before the key appears.
- Keep the key-and-box relationship concrete.

Wall inspection:

> One stone has a thin line around it.

Key discovery:

> A key inside the wall. This place was built to hide things.

Box inspection:

> The blue box has a stone keyhole.

### 11. Blue Lens Placement

#### Visible response

- The Blue Lens appears in the Compass.
- Temple Steps becomes visible on the map.
- The Compass briefly shows that the Green and Blue routes belong to the same larger pattern.

#### Dialogue

Alex:

> The cave route joins the river route here.

Mira:

> Then the Compass may be showing one connected system.

The complete nature of that system remains unresolved.

### 12. Temple Steps

#### Keep

- Stone cup.
- Dry sun channel.
- Closed gate.
- Return route to Waterfall Mouth.
- Water-cup transformation.
- Visible water paths and gate opening.

#### Add or revise

- Make the channel's carved path easy to trace visually.
- Show that the cup belongs on or near the channel without revealing the water source immediately.
- Allow Alex to connect the empty cup to the remembered waterfall.
- Give the water animation time to be seen before the next interaction becomes available.

First channel inspection:

> The channel is dry.

Second hint:

> These marks look like paths for water.

Cup inspection:

> The cup is empty. The waterfall could fill it.

During activation:

> The water is following the sun marks!

### 13. Sun Courtyard

#### Keep

- Immediate Stone Lens reward.
- Stone sun flower.
- Water already reaching the flower.
- Return path to Temple Steps.

#### Add or revise

- Stage the player's entrance so the water reaches or completes the opening of the stone flower.
- Let the Stone Lens become clearly visible at the centre.
- Do not add another puzzle between the gate and the reward.

Alex:

> The water opened the stone flower.

Optional inspection:

> It looks like Lina's flowers, but it was carved long ago.

### 14. Stone Lens Placement And Current Ending

#### Visible response

- The Stone Lens appears in the Compass.
- Green, Blue, and Stone routes converge visually.
- The Mirror Hall marker becomes visible but remains locked.
- A narrow reflected line or glint appears near the marker.
- The Compass does not yet reveal the observatory.

#### Dialogue

Mira:

> All three lenses are pointing toward the Mirror Hall.

Alex:

> But there is no sunlight inside the temple... is there?

Mira:

> That is what we need to discover next.

#### Ending purpose

The current playable experience ends with:

- three recovered lenses;
- a clearer connection between the routes;
- an unresolved question about light inside the temple;
- a strong promise for Mirror Hall;
- no premature explanation of the final observatory mechanism.

## Mirror Hall Design Direction

### Narrative purpose

Mirror Hall reveals that the temple did not create sunlight. It redirected sunlight through interior spaces toward the observatory and valley.

### Player discovery

The player should discover the room's purpose by interacting with a visible beam and observing how mirrors change its path.

### Puzzle structure

Use a clear three-part visual grammar:

1. Light source.
2. Mirror path.
3. Light destination.

### Recommended interaction sequence

1. Alex enters a dim hall with one narrow beam entering from above or through a wall opening.
2. One fixed mirror demonstrates reflection automatically.
3. The player changes the position of a small number of movable mirrors or panels.
4. Each correct change extends the visible beam farther through the room.
5. The completed beam reaches a sealed sun panel.
6. The panel opens or illuminates, revealing the Mirror Lens.
7. The beam also activates the route toward Observatory Door.

### Puzzle rules

- Keep the beam visible after every state change.
- Limit the number of mirror states.
- Make incorrect states informative rather than punitive.
- Do not reset correct mirror positions unnecessarily.
- Avoid invisible rules.
- Use spatial reasoning rather than random rotation.
- Do not require a separate unrelated key chain.

### Story reaction

Alex:

> The light was here all along. The mirrors carried it through the temple.

Mira:

> And the Compass is following the same path.

## Mirror Lens Placement

### Visible response

- The Mirror Lens joins the Compass.
- Existing lens paths become a more complete beam route.
- Observatory Door or the high observatory approach appears on the map.
- The final section remains closed until the Sun Lens is recovered.

### Dialogue

Mira:

> Four lenses show the route. One space is still dark.

Alex:

> The Sun Lens completes it.

## Sun Lens And Observatory Approach

### Narrative purpose

The Sun Lens is the final focusing component. Its sequence should test what the player has learned about connected places, light direction, and old mechanisms.

### Design requirements

- The Sun Lens should not be found in an unrelated container.
- Its puzzle should connect directly to the observatory approach.
- The player should use understanding gained from Green, Blue, Stone, and Mirror systems.
- The solution should create a visible final route to Observatory Door.
- The challenge should feel like synthesis rather than another tutorial.

### Recommended structure

1. The Mirror Hall beam reveals a high route or sun-marked mechanism.
2. The route contains one final incomplete light receiver or focusing pedestal.
3. Alex uses an environmental alignment or restored mechanism to expose the Sun Lens.
4. The Sun Lens is placed in the Compass at Base Camp.
5. All five lens paths form one complete pattern.
6. The complete Compass becomes the key or guide for Observatory Door.

## Observatory Door

### Narrative purpose

Observatory Door confirms that the complete Compass is not merely a map. It is the control or alignment device needed to reach the final mirror.

### Interaction

- The door contains a clearly matching Compass socket, pedestal, or projected pattern.
- The player uses the completed Compass once.
- The five lens colours align with the door mechanism.
- The door opens with a visible beam or rotating stone response.
- The Compass remains narratively important and is not discarded as a consumed key.

## Sun Observatory Finale

### Final player action

Alex turns or aligns the great sun mirror.

The final interaction should be simple and ceremonial. The player has already completed the reasoning challenges required to reach this point.

### Visual payoff sequence

1. Sunlight strikes the observatory mirror.
2. The mirror sends the beam toward Mirror Hall.
3. Mirror Hall redirects the light through the inner temple.
4. Temple channels and sun symbols illuminate.
5. The Sun Courtyard stone flower opens fully.
6. Light reaches the waterfall and village route.
7. Lina's garden brightens.
8. The Base Camp sun flower rises and turns toward the temple.
9. The Sun Compass glows with all five lenses.
10. The repaired map becomes complete and bright.

### Final dialogue

Alex:

> The light path is complete. Let's send it home.

After the restoration:

Lina:

> The flowers are turning toward the temple again. You brought the sunlight back.

Mira:

> You followed every path, Alex. Then you found how they belonged together.

Alex:

> The valley showed us the way.

### Final emotional meaning

The reward is not possession of the lenses. The reward is seeing the valley respond to a system the player understood and repaired.

## Environmental And Visual Language

### Route motifs

Use related visual motifs to show that locations share an origin, while reserving exact repeated symbols for shared functions.

#### General temple motif

- Sun rays.
- Circular stone forms.
- Flower-like geometry.
- Warm gold and stone colours.

#### Water-channel motif

- Carved flowing lines.
- Blue or darkened grooves when inactive.
- Bright reflective lines when active.

#### Light-route motif

- Straight rays.
- Mirror marks.
- Small circular receivers.
- Bright points showing source and destination.

#### Map-route motif

- Lens-coloured paths.
- Lines appearing only after relevant lens placement.
- Clear disabled styling for locations not yet reachable.

### Symbol rule

Use an identical symbol only when it communicates an identical or closely related gameplay function. Decorative continuity should use variations rather than exact copies.

## Puzzle Design Rules

### Required qualities

- Every required object has a visible or inferable purpose.
- Obstacles are understandable before their solutions are available.
- Previously visited places may become useful again.
- Backtracking rewards memory and should not exist only to extend playtime.
- State changes remain visible after completion.
- Incorrect actions provide warm, useful feedback.
- Puzzles do not punish experimentation.
- A solution should produce a proportionate visible response.

### Puzzle variety

Use the existing sequence as a progression in thinking:

| Sequence | Primary thinking skill |
| --- | --- |
| Restore map | Combine matching parts |
| Repair bridge | Match a practical tool to an obstacle |
| Return basket | Recognize ownership and help a character |
| Make glow jar | Combine properties of two objects |
| Open blue box | Follow an environmental clue to a lock-and-key relationship |
| Fill temple channel | Remember and reuse a previous location |
| Align Mirror Hall | Predict spatial cause and effect |
| Reach observatory | Combine knowledge from multiple earlier systems |
| Turn final mirror | Perform a clear concluding action and observe the world payoff |

### Interaction clarity

- Avoid repeated `look` actions when a more intentional verb or state change is appropriate.
- Show parts of hidden objects when the player is meant to search nearby.
- Distinguish scenery from interactive mechanisms.
- Do not introduce false visual codes.
- Keep clickable areas aligned with visible objects.

## Map And Hub Progression

### Base Camp function

Base Camp remains the emotional and mechanical hub.

Every major return should provide at least one meaningful change:

- Compass progress.
- New map path.
- Updated understanding.
- Flower or light response.
- New optional conversation.

### Map progression

| Progress state | Map response |
| --- | --- |
| Torn map restored | Jungle Path becomes available |
| Green Lens placed | Waterfall region appears |
| Blue Lens placed | Temple Steps appears |
| Stone Lens placed | Mirror Hall appears as the next mystery |
| Mirror Lens placed | Observatory approach appears |
| Sun Lens placed | Final route and Observatory Door activate |
| Observatory restored | Map becomes complete and bright |

### Locked-location communication

A visible but unavailable location must communicate that it belongs to the next stage of the adventure, not that the interface is broken.

Use:

- a distinct future-path visual state;
- concise story text;
- a clear connection to the most recent discovery.

## Content Boundaries

Do not add:

- A villain introduced only to create stakes.
- Combat, weapons, or frightening guardians.
- A countdown or catastrophic threat.
- A chosen-one explanation for Alex.
- Long temple-lore monologues.
- Random collectible currencies.
- Inventory objects with no later purpose.
- Grammar questions as gates for core story progress.
- A quiz after every discovery.
- Extra NPC errands that do not affect a relationship or world state.
- A progression system that competes with the five lenses.
- Puzzle hints that reveal solutions before the player has explored.
- An additional puzzle between a major mechanism and its promised reward.

## Content Generation Requirements For LLMs

When generating new scene content, dialogue, or puzzle specifications:

1. Identify the scene's immediate obstacle.
2. Identify what the player can observe before acting.
3. Identify what prior knowledge or location the solution uses.
4. Define the visible state change produced by the solution.
5. Define one concise required clue.
6. Define optional flavour separately from required information.
7. Preserve Alex's curious, practical voice.
8. Preserve Mira's partial and evolving understanding.
9. Connect the scene to water, light, growth, mapping, or temple mechanisms only when the connection is physically legible.
10. Avoid adding an item unless it has a necessary and visible use.
11. Avoid explaining a later revelation early.
12. Ensure success changes the world, a relationship, or the player's understanding.

## Final Design Test

Every proposed addition should pass this test:

> Does this help the child observe, understand, choose, care about, or visibly repair the valley?

The complete adventure should leave the player with the feeling that careful observation and small acts of help can reveal how a larger world fits together.
